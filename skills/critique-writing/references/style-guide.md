# Style Guide

Loose guidelines for blog posts and essays. Break any rule rather than write something ugly.

## Voice

- First person. Say "I" not "we" (unless genuinely plural).
- Contractions are fine. Write as if addressing a literate friend — not a professor, not a crowd.
- Consistent tone within a piece. Don't swing between formal and casual without reason.
- Classic style: writing is *showing*, not arguing or performing. You've noticed something; orient the reader's gaze so they see it too.
- Conceal the effort. Don't apologize, narrate your struggle, or confess uncertainty about your enterprise.

## Sentences

- Short default. One idea per sentence. If a sentence needs a semicolon, consider splitting it.
- Vary length for rhythm — a short sentence after a long one creates emphasis.
- Active voice unless passive is genuinely clearer. "The server dropped the connection" not "the connection was dropped." Use passive deliberately to control what the reader attends to, not out of habit.
- Right-branching. Main clause first, modifiers trail behind. Don't front-load qualifiers before the verb — the reader has to hold too much in memory.
- Keep subject near verb, verb near object. Long parenthetical insertions between them are the most common source of hard-to-read sentences.
- State what something IS, not what it ISN'T. Negation takes longer to process. Use it only when the negation is the point.

## Paragraphs

- Lead with the point. Bury it and the reader may never find it. (Topic, then comment.)
- Given, then new. Start each sentence with what the reader already knows, then introduce new information. This chains sentences together.
- Cap at 4–5 sentences. If longer, it probably contains two ideas.
- Each paragraph earns the next. If the reader could skip one without losing the thread, cut it.

## Word choice

- Concrete over abstract. "Latency dropped from 200ms to 40ms" not "performance improved significantly."
- Prefer the short, plain word. "Before" not "prior to." "Use" not "utilize." "To" not "in order to."
- Cut filler. "It's worth noting that" → delete. "Basically" → delete. "It is important to note" → delete.
- No hedging without purpose. "Might", "perhaps", "seems like" — commit or qualify with evidence.
- Kill zombie nouns. Nominalizations (-tion, -ment, -ance) drain life from prose and hide the actor. "We affirmed" not "we effected an affirmation." Convert back to verbs.
- Jargon: use when your audience shares it, define on first use when they don't. Beware of chunking — experts compress concepts into single terms the reader hasn't built yet. Unpack them.
- Don't editorialize with adjectives. Don't tell the reader something is "interesting" or "controversial" — show it through evidence.
- Refuse euphemisms. Call things what they are.
- If you use a metaphor, maintain it. Mixed metaphors clash in the reader's imagery. Dead metaphors ("field of study") are fine.

## Structure

- Information as a DAG. Concepts before they're needed. No forward references to undefined terms.
- Headings are promises. Every section delivers what the heading claims.
- Openings earn attention. No throat-clearing ("In this post, I will discuss..."). No metadiscourse about the document itself.
- Closings land. No trailing off. End when you're done.
- Use connectives. Err on the side of too many (but, however, because, therefore) over too few. They tell the reader how the current sentence relates to the previous one.
- Read aloud. If it sounds stiff, rewrite. If it sounds slangy, dial back.

## Code in prose

- Surrounding text matches what the code does. If the code changes, update the prose.
- State prerequisites. What does the reader need installed/configured?
- Runnable examples. If the reader pastes it, it works.

## The curse of knowledge

Your biggest enemy. You can't imagine not knowing what you know, so you assume the reader shares your context.

- Show drafts to someone who isn't you.
- When you say something abstract, follow it with a concrete instance.
- Do a revision pass where you add no new content — only make every sentence comprehensible to someone who isn't you.
